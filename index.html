CAS Physical Intelligence Team

We are the Physical Intelligence Team at the Chinese Academy of Sciences (CAS). Our mission is to build intelligent, reliable, and practically deployable robots by bridging geometry, physics, and learning-based control.

Our research interests include embodied intelligence, physical & spatial intelligence, robotic manipulation and navigation, world modeling, neural simulators, and learning with physical priors. We aim to develop systems that generalize across tasks and environments, and can be validated in real-world industrial scenarios.

PI / Leadership

Kai Xu (徐凯) is a Professor at the Institute of AI for Industries (IAII), Chinese Academy of Sciences. Before joining IAII, he was a professor at the National University of Defense Technology (NUDT). He used to be an Adjunct Professor of Simon Fraser University. He earned his Ph.D. in Computer Science in 2011 from NUDT.

From 2008 to 2010, he worked as a visiting PhD student in the GrUVi Lab at Simon Fraser University, supervised by Richard (Hao) Zhang. During 2012–2014, he conducted postdoctoral research at VCC@SIAT with Baoquan Chen. During 2017–2018, he visited the Vision and Robotics Group at Princeton University, working with Thomas Funkhouser and Szymon Rusinkiewicz. (Homepage)

Kai is leading the PAI Lab @ IAII and co-leading the iGRAPE Lab @ NUDT. His research spans computer graphics, embodied intelligence, and physical & spatial intelligence, with current interests in world modeling, neural simulators, and robotic manipulation and navigation.

Research

We study physical intelligence by integrating physical priors with data-driven methods, enabling robust perception–decision–control loops under contact-rich interactions and deformation.

From Specialist to Generalist: A Comprehensive Survey on World Models
Kai Xu*, Hang Zhao, Ruizhen Hu, Yuhang Huang, Ziqiao Zhou, Wancheng Feng, Li Yi, Sida Peng, Xing Liu, Zihao Liu, Jin Zhang, Chenyang Zhu, Renjiao Yi, Qin Zou, Bo Du, Haibin Yu
Submitted, 2026
Paper

This survey analyzes world models through the lens of a fundamental trade-off, arguing that specialist and generalist paradigms must coexist due to competing needs for precision and generalization. Reconciling this tension is essential for progress toward artificial general intelligence (AGI).
